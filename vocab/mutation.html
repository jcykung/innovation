Mutation

Breaking Down the Word: A Morphemic Analysis

mutare+-ation

Mutare: A Latin word meaning "to change."

-ation: A suffix indicating an action or process.

So, a "mutation" is literally the process of a change occurring.

The Core Idea: A Change in the Genetic Code

A mutation is a change in the DNA sequence of an organism. It's like a "typo" in the genetic instruction manual. Mutations can happen randomly when a cell divides, or they can be caused by environmental factors like radiation or certain chemicals.

Are Mutations Positive, Negative, or Neutral?

Most mutations have no effect at all! But they can also be negative or, very rarely, positive. They are the ultimate source of all genetic variation.

Negative

A mutation can cause a protein to work incorrectly or not at all, leading to a genetic disorder like cystic fibrosis.

Neutral

The mutation changes the DNA, but it doesn't change the final protein, or the change is so small it doesn't matter. Most mutations are neutral.

Positive

Rarely, a mutation can give an organism a survival advantage, like bacteria developing antibiotic resistance. This is the raw material for evolution.

Reading in Context: The Peppered Moth

Before the 1800s in England, most peppered moths were light-coloured, which camouflaged them against lichen-covered trees. A rare mutation in a single gene caused some moths to have a dark-coloured (melanic) phenotype. This mutation made the dark moths easy for birds to spot, so they were very rare.

When the Industrial Revolution began, soot from factories blackened the trees, killing the light-coloured lichen. Suddenly, the light-coloured moths were easily seen, while the dark moths were camouflaged. The mutation that was once negative became positive, giving the dark moths a survival advantage. This is a classic example of natural selection acting on a random mutation.
