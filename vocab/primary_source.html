Primary Source

Breaking Down the Term

Primary+Source

Primary: From the Latin "primus," meaning "first."

Source: From the Latin "surgere" (to rise), referring to "the point of origin" (like a spring of water).

So, a "primary source" is the first or original place where information comes from.

The Core Idea: First-Hand Evidence

A primary source is a piece of evidence that was created at the time of the event by someone who was there. It is an original record that has not been filtered or interpreted by a historian yet. It is the raw material of history.

Primary vs. Secondary Sources

The main difference is when it was created and who created it.

Primary Sources

"I was there."

  • Diaries & Journals
  • Photographs & Videos
  • Letters & Emails
  • Artifacts (clothing, tools)
  • Official Records (birth certificates)

Secondary Sources

"I heard/read about it."

  • History Textbooks
  • Biographies (written later)
  • Documentaries
  • Encyclopedia Articles
  • Teacher's Lectures

Reading in Context: The Time Capsule

A group of students discovered a dusty metal box buried near the school's foundation stone. It was a time capsule from 1950. Inside, they found a newspaper from that day, a baseball card, a handwritten letter from a student named 'Billy', and a black-and-white photograph of the class.

Their teacher explained, "Everything in this box is a primary source. That letter gives us Billy's direct thoughts from 1950, unfiltered by time. The newspaper shows us exactly what people were reading that morning." She held up their history textbook. "This book tells us about the 1950s, but it was written in 2020 by historians who looked at things like this box. That makes the textbook a secondary source. The items in the capsule are the raw evidence; the textbook is the interpretation."
