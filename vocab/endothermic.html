Endothermic

Breaking Down the Word

Endo-+Thermic

Endo-: A Greek prefix meaning "inside," "inner," or "within." (Think of "enter" or "endoskeleton").

Thermic: From the Greek word "therme," meaning "heat."

So, "endothermic" literally describes a process that takes heat inside.

The Core Idea: Absorbing Energy

An endothermic reaction is a chemical reaction that absorbs energy from its surroundings, usually in the form of heat. Because the reaction is "stealing" heat from the environment to fuel itself, the temperature of the surroundings drops.

Recognizing Endothermic Reactions

How do you know if a reaction is endothermic? Look for these signs.

Temperature Drop

This is the most common sign. If you touch the container and it feels cold, the reaction is absorbing heat energy from your hand.

Requires Heat

Some reactions won't start or continue unless you constantly supply heat (like baking a cake or melting ice).

Common Examples

  • Melting ice (Phase change)
  • Photosynthesis (Plants absorbing sun energy)
  • Instant cold packs (Chemical reaction)
  • Cooking an egg (Denaturing proteins)

Note: The opposite is Exothermic (exo = outside), where heat is released, making the surroundings feel hot.

Reading in Context: The Instant Cold Pack

During a soccer game, a player twisted their ankle. The coach immediately grabbed an "instant cold pack" from the first aid kit. He squeezed the pack firmly to break a small inner water bag, shook it, and placed it on the player's ankle.

The pack instantly became freezing cold. This wasn't because it had been in a freezer; it was pure chemistry. Inside the pack, solid ammonium nitrate crystals mixed with the water. Dissolving this specific chemical is an endothermic process. It requires a lot of energy to break the bonds of the crystals. The reaction absorbed that heat energy from the water and the player's ankle, leaving the pack (and the injury) incredibly cold.
