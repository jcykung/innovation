Natural Selection

Breaking Down the Term

Natural+Selection

Natural: From the Latin "natura," meaning "nature, birth, or course of things."

Selection: From the Latin "seligere," meaning "to choose or select."

So, "natural selection" is the process where nature "chooses" which individuals are best suited to survive and reproduce.

The Core Idea: Survival of the "Fittest"

Natural selection is the process by which organisms that are better adapted to their environment tend to survive and produce more offspring. "Fittest" doesn't mean strongest or fastest—it means the best fit for a specific environment.

The Three Key Principles

Natural selection happens in three main steps, driven by the environment.

1. Variation

Individuals within a population have different traits (e.g., different colours). This variation originally comes from random mutations.

2. Selection

An environmental pressure (like a predator) makes survival difficult. Individuals with advantageous traits (like green camouflage) are more likely to survive and have more offspring.

3. Inheritance

The advantageous traits are passed on to the next generation. Over time, the "selected" trait becomes more common in the population as it adapts.

Reading in Context: Darwin's Finches

On the Galápagos Islands, Charles Darwin observed that different islands had finches with very different beaks. On one island, a severe drought (the selection pressure) wiped out the plants with small, soft seeds, leaving only large, tough seeds.

The finches on that island had natural variation in their beak size. The birds with smaller, weaker beaks could not crack the tough seeds and starved. However, the birds with a random mutation for larger, stronger beaks were able to eat and survive. This process is natural selection. These survivors reproduced, passing on their genes for large beaks. Over many generations, the average beak size of the finches on that island increased.
