Ethical Dilemma

Breaking Down the Term

Ethical+Dilemma

Ethical: From the Greek "ethikos," relating to moral character (what is right or wrong).

Dilemma: From the Greek "di" (two) + "lemma" (premise). It literally means "two propositions."

So, an "ethical dilemma" is a situation where you must choose between two difficult moral options.

The Core Idea: A Choice Between "Rights"

A true ethical dilemma isn't just a choice between right and wrong (that's usually easy, even if hard to do). It is a choice between two "rights." You have two values that are important to you, but in this specific situation, they conflict. You cannot follow both.

Common Types of Dilemmas

Ethical dilemmas often involve clashing values. Philosopher Rushworth Kidder identified four common patterns:

Truth vs. Loyalty

Should you tell the truth about a friend's mistake, or stay loyal and protect them?

Individual vs. Community

Should you protect the rights of one person, or the safety of the entire group?

Short-term vs. Long-term

Should you take a benefit now (like a quick profit), or wait for a better result in the future?

Justice vs. Mercy

Should you apply the rules fairly to everyone (justice), or make an exception for someone in trouble (mercy)?

Reading in Context: The Final Exam

Sam was taking a difficult final history exam. He looked up and saw his best friend, Alex, using a small cheat sheet hidden under the desk. Sam knew the school's honour code required students to report any cheating they witnessed. If he didn't report it, he was technically breaking the rules too.

Sam faced a classic ethical dilemma of Truth vs. Loyalty. He valued Truth and fairness; it wasn't right that Alex might get a better grade than students who studied hard. However, he also valued Loyalty to his friend and didn't want Alex to get suspended. There was no "good" outcome where everyone ended up happy. He had to weigh which value was more important in that specific moment.

Historical Context (WWI): Fritz Haber's Choice

Fritz Haber was a brilliant German chemist who faced a profound ethical dilemma during World War I. He invented a process to synthesize ammonia, which created fertilizers that fed billions. However, he chose to use his genius to develop chlorine gasâ€”the first chemical weapon.

Haber's conflict fit the pattern of Short-term vs. Long-term. He believed the short-term benefit of using a terrifying weapon would end the war quickly and save lives. However, he ignored the long-term consequence: unleashing a horrific new form of warfare on humanity. His story illustrates how prioritizing an immediate "win" can lead to lasting devastation.

Historical Context (Early 20th Century): The Eugenics Movement

In the early 1900s, many governments, including Canada's, supported Eugenics. They believed they faced an ethical dilemma of Individual vs. Community. They argued that to improve the "community" (the human race) and reduce future suffering, they should prevent people deemed "unfit" from having children.

This led to forced sterilization laws. This "dilemma" was based on a dangerous interpretation of the utilitarian good. They sacrificed the fundamental rights of the individual for a theoretical, perfected community. History has judged this as a failure of ethics, showing that collective goals should never trample basic human rights.

Historical Context (WWII): Japanese Internment

During World War II, the Canadian government faced a massive ethical dilemma: Individual vs. Community. The government feared that Japanese-Canadians might act as spies, threatening the safety of the "community" (the nation).

To protect national security, they forcibly moved over 20,000 Japanese-Canadians into internment camps, stripping them of their individual rights, homes, and businesses. Today, this is viewed as a grave injustice. The government allowed fear to outweigh the rights of innocent citizens, proving that the need for "community safety" must be balanced carefully against individual liberty.

Future Context: CRISPR and Gene Editing

Today, we face a new ethical dilemma with CRISPR technology, which allows us to edit DNA. This presents a conflict of Short-term vs. Long-term. In the short-term, we could cure devastating genetic diseases in babies before they are born, preventing a lifetime of suffering.

However, these edits are passed down forever, changing the human genome in the long-term. If we start editing for health, will we eventually edit for height or intelligence? We must decide if the immediate good of curing a specific patient outweighs the long-term risks of permanently altering the human species.
