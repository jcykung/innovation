Phenotype

Breaking Down the Word: A Morphemic Analysis

phaino-+-type

Phaino-: From the Greek word "phainein," meaning "to show" or "to appear."

-type: From the Greek word "typos," meaning "mark" or "model."

So, a "phenotype" is the "type that shows" or the model that is physically expressed.

The Core Idea: The Trait You Can See

The phenotype is the observable, physical characteristic of an organism. It's what you can see with your eyes, like hair colour, height, eye colour, or flower colour. It's the result of the genotype interacting with the environment.

Genotype vs. Phenotype: The Code vs. The Result

This is the most important distinction in genetics. The genotype is the instruction, and the phenotype is the final product.

Genotype (The Genetic Code)

The specific combination of alleles.
Examples: BB, Bb, bb

Phenotype (The Physical Trait)

The observable characteristic that is expressed.
Examples: Brown Eyes, Blue Eyes

Notice how different genotypes (BB and Bb) can result in the same phenotype (Brown Eyes) because the 'B' allele is dominant.

Reading in Context: Mendel's Pea Plants

A student was recreating Gregor Mendel's famous experiments. She crossed a tall pea plant with a short pea plant. All the offspring were tall. "The tall phenotype is clearly dominant," she noted in her lab book. Even though all the new plants looked tall, she knew their genotype was different from the tall parent's.

She then crossed two of these new tall plants together. In the next generation, she saw that about three-quarters of the plants had a tall phenotype, but one-quarter had a short phenotype! The short trait had reappeared. This proved that the first generation of tall plants had a heterozygous genotype (Tt), which allowed the short phenotype (tt) to show up again later.
