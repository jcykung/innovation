The Core Idea: Support for a Claim

Evidence is the collection of facts, signs, or information that helps you believe something is true or show that a claim is valid. It's the "support" that connects a statement to reality.

Evidence in Different Fields

While the core idea is the same, what counts as good evidence can change depending on the subject.

In Science: Empirical Data

Scientific evidence must be observable, measurable, and repeatable. It comes from controlled experiments, sensory observation, and data analysis.

Example: Bubbles forming when mixing two chemicals is evidence of a chemical reaction.

In History: Traces of the Past

Historical evidence relies on sources left behind by people. We cannot "repeat" history in a lab, so we rely on documents, artifacts, and oral traditions to reconstruct what happened.

Example: An ancient Roman coin found in India is evidence that Rome and India traded with each other.

What Is Not Strong Evidence?

It's important to distinguish evidence from things that can't support a claim on their own:

  • Personal Opinion: "I think chocolate is the best flavour." (This is a preference, not a provable fact).
  • A Feeling or Hunch: "I just have a bad feeling about this." (This isn't based on external facts).
  • An Anecdote (a single story): "My grandpa smoked his whole life and lived to be 90." (This one story doesn't disprove the scientific evidence about the dangers of smoking).

Reading in Context: The Case of the Missing Mascot

The school's beloved mascot, a giant stuffed eagle named Captain Talon, had vanished from the gym. The principal announced that the school's "Junior Detective Club" would handle the investigation. The first thing the club did was look for evidence. They found a trail of muddy footprints leading from an open window to the empty display case. This was their first solid piece of evidence, suggesting how the culprit got in.

Next, they found a small piece of blue fabric snagged on the corner of the display case. They recognized it as being from the rival school's team jacket. While this seemed like strong evidence, the club president reminded everyone that it wasn't enough to make an accusation. "We need more than just one piece of circumstantial evidence," she said. "We need to collect all the facts before we can be sure." The hunt for more evidence continued, with the club determined to follow where the facts led them.
