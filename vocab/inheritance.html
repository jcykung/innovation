The Core Idea: Passing Traits to the Next Generation

Genetic inheritance is the process by which parents pass on their genes to their children. Each child inherits a unique combination of genes from their parents, which is why family members often look similar, but are never identical (unless they are identical twins).

The Rules of Inheritance

For many traits, inheritance follows predictable patterns discovered by Gregor Mendel.

Alleles: Different Versions of a Gene

For any given gene (like for eye colour), you inherit one version from each parent. These different versions are called alleles. For example, there are alleles for brown eyes and alleles for blue eyes.

Dominant vs. Recessive Alleles

Some alleles are dominant, meaning you only need to inherit one copy for that trait to show up. Other alleles are recessive, meaning you need to inherit two copies for the trait to appear. The brown eye allele (B) is dominant over the blue eye allele (b).

Genotype vs. Phenotype

Genotype is the actual set of alleles an organism has (e.g., BB, Bb, or bb). The phenotype is the observable physical trait that results from the genotype (e.g., brown eyes or blue eyes).

Reading in Context: The Family Reunion

At the family reunion, Leo was looking at an old black and white photo of his great-grandparents. He noticed that his great-grandfather had a distinctive widow's peak hairline, just like his grandfather and his own dad. "It looks like you all got that from him," Leo's mom said. "That's a classic example of genetic inheritance. The gene for a widow's peak is dominant."

Leo, who didn't have a widow's peak, was confused. "If it's dominant, why don't I have it?" His mom smiled. "Because of your inheritance from me! You get half your genes from Dad and half from me. I have a straight hairline, which is a recessive trait. You must have received a recessive allele from Dad and one from me, so the widow's peak didn't show up for you. Your genetic lottery was just different."
