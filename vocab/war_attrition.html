War of Attrition

Breaking Down the Word

ad-+terere

Ad-: A Latin prefix meaning "to" or "at."

Terere: A Latin verb meaning "to rub" or "to grind."

So, "attrition" literally means the action of rubbing things together until they wear down.

The Core Idea: The Grinding Match

A war of attrition is a military strategy where one side attempts to win not by capturing territory or winning big battles, but by wearing down the enemy to the point of collapse. The goal is to kill more soldiers and destroy more supplies than the enemy can replace, until they simply give up or run out of men.

Why WWI Became a War of Attrition

In World War I, neither side could win a quick victory. Several factors created a deadly deadlock.

1. The Stalemate

Powerful defensive weapons like machine guns made it nearly impossible to attack successfully. Armies dug trenches to survive, stopping movement completely.

2. Industrial Power

Factories could produce shells and bullets faster than soldiers could use them. This meant the fighting could continue endlessly without running out of ammo.

3. Lack of Strategy

Generals didn't know how to break the trench lines. Their only solution was to throw more men at the problem, hoping the enemy would run out of men first.

4. Total War

Attrition targeted the whole nation. Blockades were used to starve the enemy civilians so they could no longer support the army.

Reading in Context: The Battle of Verdun (1916)

The Battle of Verdun is the most terrifying example of a war of attrition. The German General Erich von Falkenhayn came up with a brutal plan. He knew the French would defend the fortress city of Verdun to the last man because of its symbolic importance. He didn't actually want to capture the city quickly.

Instead, Falkenhayn stated his goal was to "bleed France white." He planned to attack Verdun just enough to force the French to pour all their soldiers into the battle, where German artillery would destroy them. He believed that for every German soldier lost, two or three French soldiers would die. The battle lasted for 300 days. By the end, there were over 700,000 casualties (dead and wounded) combined. The front lines had barely moved. The "strategy" of attrition resulted only in mass slaughter without a decisive victory.
