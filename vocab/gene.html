The Core Idea: A Single Instruction in the DNA Manual

A gene is a specific segment of a DNA molecule that contains the instructions for building one specific protein. Think of it as a single recipe in a giant cookbook. This protein then goes on to perform a specific job, resulting in a physical trait like your hair colour or a functional trait like your blood type.

The Central Dogma: How a Gene Works

Genes don't create traits directly. They work through a two-step process to build proteins, which are the real workers in our bodies.

1. Transcription: Making a Copy

The cell makes a temporary copy of the gene's instructions. This copy is called messenger RNA (mRNA).

2. Translation: Building the Protein

The mRNA copy travels out of the nucleus to a cellular machine called a ribosome, which reads the instructions and assembles the protein piece by piece.

Reading in Context: The Blue-Eyed Surprise

David and his partner both have brown eyes, so they were surprised when their daughter, Emily, was born with bright blue eyes. "How is that possible?" David asked their doctor. The doctor explained, "It's all about which gene versions she inherited from you both. The gene for brown eyes is dominant, while the one for blue eyes is recessive."

"Because you both have brown eyes, we know you each have at least one copy of the brown eye gene," she continued. "But you must also both secretly carry the recessive gene for blue eyes. Emily just happened to be the lucky one who inherited the blue-eyed gene from both of you, so that's the trait she shows."
