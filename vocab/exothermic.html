Exothermic

Breaking Down the Word

Exo-+Thermic

Exo-: A Greek prefix meaning "outside," "outer," or "external." (Think of "exit" or "exoskeleton").

Thermic: From the Greek word "therme," meaning "heat."

So, "exothermic" literally describes a process that sends heat outside.

The Core Idea: Releasing Energy

An exothermic reaction is a chemical reaction that releases energy into its surroundings, usually in the form of heat or light. Because the energy is leaving the reaction and going into the surrounding area, the temperature of the surroundings goes up.

Recognizing Exothermic Reactions

How do you know if a reaction is exothermic? Look for these signs.

Temperature Rise

This is the most common sign. If you touch the container and it feels hot, energy is being released.

Light Production

Fire (combustion) is a classic example. The reaction releases energy so intensely that you see it as light and feel it as heat.

Common Examples

  • Burning wood (Combustion)
  • Hand warmers (Oxidation of Iron)
  • Cellular Respiration (How your body makes energy)
  • Concrete setting (Hardening)

Note: The opposite is Endothermic (endo = inside), where heat is absorbed, making the surroundings feel cold.

Reading in Context: The Hand Warmer

It was a freezing cold morning at the bus stop. Sarah pulled a disposable hand warmer out of its plastic package and gave it a shake. Within minutes, the small packet was delightfully hot.

Sarah knew this wasn't magic; it was chemistry. Inside the packet were iron filings, water, and salt. When she opened the package, air rushed in. The oxygen in the air reacted with the iron to form rust. This rusting process is an exothermic reaction. While rust usually forms slowly on an old car, the ingredients in the packet made it happen super fast. The "waste product" of this reaction was heat energy, which was exactly what Sarah needed to warm her frozen fingers.
