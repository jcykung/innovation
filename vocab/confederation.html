Confederation

Breaking Down the Word: A Morphemic Analysis

con-+foedus+-ation

Con-: A Latin prefix meaning "with" or "together."

Foedus: A Latin word meaning a "league," "treaty," or "alliance."

-ation: A suffix indicating an action or process.

So, "confederation" is literally the process of coming together to form a league or alliance.

The Core Idea: A Country of United Provinces

A confederation is an act where a number of separate colonies, provinces, or states agree to unite under a single central government. While they become one country, they each keep specific powers to govern themselves locally.

The Canadian Context: Creating a Nation

In Canada, "Confederation" specifically refers to the historic event on July 1, 1867, when the British North American colonies of Canada (which split into Ontario and Quebec), Nova Scotia, and New Brunswick were united into one country, the Dominion of Canada.

Key Drivers of Confederation

  • Political Deadlock: The Province of Canada (Canada West and Canada East) was politically unstable and couldn't form lasting governments.
  • Economic Reasons: A railway linking the colonies was needed to increase trade and economic prosperity.
  • Defence: There was a fear of American expansion northward, especially after their Civil War. A united country would be easier to defend.

The Fathers of Confederation

The politicians and delegates who attended the conferences in Charlottetown (1864), Quebec City (1864), and London (1866) to negotiate the terms of union are known as the Fathers of Confederation.

Reading in Context: A Debate at the Quebec Conference, 1864

John A. Macdonald stood and addressed the delegates. "Gentlemen, we are at a crossroads. We can remain as separate, weak colonies, or we can choose confederation and build a great nation. A central government is essential for our defence and for the railway that will bind us together. This is the only path forward."

A delegate from Nova Scotia rose to his feet. "Mr. Macdonald, we are not against the idea of confederation in principle, but we fear losing our identity. Will our local needs be ignored? Will this new federal government overpower us?" George-Étienne Cartier of Canada East then spoke. "That is why this confederation must protect provincial rights. Quebec must maintain its unique culture and language. A successful confederation will be a partnership, strengthening us all while respecting our differences."
