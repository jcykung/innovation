Artificial Selection

Breaking Down the Term

Artificial+Selection

Artificial: From the Latin "artificium," meaning "craft, skill, or art." It means something made by humans, not by nature.

Selection: The act of choosing which individuals will reproduce.

So, "artificial selection" is the process where humans "choose" which individuals get to reproduce to get desired traits.

Natural Selection vs. Artificial Selection

The process is similar (variation, selection, inheritance), but the "selector" and the "goal" are different.

Natural Selection

  • Selector: The Environment (predators, climate, food availability)
  • Goal: Survival & Reproduction.
  • Result: Well-adapted organisms.

Artificial Selection

  • Selector: Humans.
  • Goal: Desired Traits (e.g., bigger fruit, specific dog appearance).
  • Result: Organisms useful to humans.

Reading in Context: The Wild Mustard Plant

Thousands of years ago, farmers began cultivating a single plant: the wild mustard. This plant had natural variation in its traits. By using artificial selection, farmers created a huge variety of common vegetables by choosing which plants to breed, generation after generation.

Farmers who selected and bred plants with large, tender leaves eventually created kale and collard greens. Farmers who selected for enlarged, edible buds created Brussels sprouts. By selecting for large, dense flower clusters, farmers created broccoli and cauliflower. All of these very different vegetables are the result of artificial selection from the exact same ancestor plant.
