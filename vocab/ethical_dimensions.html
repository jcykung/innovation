Ethical Dimensions

Breaking Down the Terms

Ethical+Dimensions

Ethical: From the Greek "ethikos," referring to morals, character, and right vs. wrong.

Dimensions: Aspects or features of a situation.

So, "Ethical Dimensions" are the parts of history where we make moral judgments about the past.

The Core Idea: Judging the Past Fairly

History isn't just about facts; it involves real people who made difficult choices. The concept of Ethical Dimensions asks us to judge historical actions, but to do so carefully. We must avoid imposing our modern standards instantly ("presentism") while still recognizing actions that were universally unjust or cruel.

How to Make Ethical Judgments

We don't study the past just to say "they were bad." We study it to understand human nature and our responsibilities today.

1. Understanding vs. Approval

You can try to understand why someone did something terrible (the context) without approving of what they did.

2. Crimes vs. Mistakes

Was the action accepted at the time but seen as wrong now? Or was it considered a crime or an atrocity even back then?

3. Responsibility

Who is responsible? The leader who gave the order, the soldiers who followed it, or the public who stayed silent?

4. Meaning for Today

How should we remember this? Should we build statues, offer apologies, or pay reparations? This connects history to the present.

Reading in Context: The Statue Debate

In recent years, many Canadians have debated whether to remove statues of Sir John A. Macdonald, Canada's first Prime Minister. This is a classic issue of the Ethical Dimension.

On one hand, Macdonald was a key architect of Confederation, helping to create the country we live in today. In the context of the 1860s, he was a successful political leader.
