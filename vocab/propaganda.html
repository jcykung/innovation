Propaganda

Breaking Down the Terms

Propagare

Propagare: A Latin verb meaning "to spread," "to increase," or "to multiply." (Related to 'propagate' in gardening).

History: Originally used by the Catholic Church in the 1600s ("Congregation for Propagating the Faith").

So, "propaganda" is information spread specifically to promote a cause or point of view.

The Core Idea: Persuasion Over Truth

Propaganda is communication used to influence the opinions and behaviors of large numbers of people. Unlike education, which aims to present facts objectively, propaganda is biased. It appeals to emotions (fear, pride, anger) rather than logic to steer the audience toward a specific conclusion.

Aims of Canadian War Propaganda

During the World Wars, the Canadian government used propaganda with three specific goals in mind to ensure victory.

1. Recruit Soldiers

Posters played on a sense of duty, pride, or guilt to convince young men to enlist immediately.

2. Support the War Effort

Civilians were urged to buy Victory Bonds and ration food. It made ordinary actions feel heroic and essential.

3. Vilify the Enemy

Propaganda depicted the enemy as terrifying monsters or barbarians to provoke anger and justify the fighting.

Reading in Context: Recruitment in WWI

During World War I, Britain (and Canada) needed millions of men to volunteer for the army. The government used propaganda posters to manipulate men's sense of pride, duty, and shame.

One famous poster showed a father sitting in an armchair after the war, while his daughter asks, "Daddy, what did YOU do in the Great War?" The father looks away, silent and ashamed. This used the technique of Emotional Appeal (Guilt). It didn't mention the low pay or the deadly trenches. Instead, it bypassed logic to target a deep personal fear: the fear of future embarrassment in front of one's own family. It effectively shamed thousands of men into enlisting.
