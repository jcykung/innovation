Genotype

Breaking Down the Word: A Morphemic Analysis

geno-+-type

Geno-: From the Greek word "genos," meaning "origin" or "generation."

-type: From the Greek word "typos," meaning "mark" or "model."

So, a "genotype" is the specific genetic model or blueprint an organism inherits.

The Core Idea: Your Specific Genetic Code

The genotype is the specific combination of alleles (gene versions) that an individual has for a particular gene. It's the "behind-the-scenes" genetic information written in your DNA. You can't see a genotype just by looking at someone.

Genotype vs. Phenotype: The Code vs. The Result

This is the most important distinction in genetics. The genotype is the instruction, and the phenotype is the final product.

Genotype (The Genetic Code)

The specific combination of alleles.
Examples: BB, Bb, bb

Phenotype (The Physical Trait)

The observable characteristic that is expressed.
Examples: Brown Eyes, Blue Eyes

Notice how different genotypes (BB and Bb) can result in the same phenotype (Brown Eyes) because the 'B' allele is dominant.

Reading in Context: The Chocolate Lab

Anna's family breeds Labrador retrievers. Their black lab, Shadow, just had puppies with another black lab. Most of the puppies were black, but one was chocolate brown! A friend asked how that was possible. "It's all in their genotype," Anna explained. "In labs, the black coat allele (B) is dominant over the brown coat allele (b)."

"Both parent dogs are black, so that's their phenotype. But for them to have a brown puppy, their genotype must be Bb. They each carry the recessive brown allele," she said. "The brown puppy inherited the 'b' allele from both of them, giving it a 'bb' genotype, which is the only way to get a chocolate-coloured phenotype."
