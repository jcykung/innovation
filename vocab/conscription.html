Conscription

Breaking Down the Word

Con-+Scribere

Con-: A Latin prefix meaning "with" or "together."

Scribere: A Latin verb meaning "to write."

Literally, it means "writing names together on a list."

The Core Idea: Compulsory Service

Conscription (often called "the draft") is the mandatory enlistment of people in a national service, usually the military. It happens when a government passes a law requiring citizens to fight, rather than relying on volunteers.

The Great Debate

Conscription is always controversial because it clashes two important values: National Security vs. Individual Freedom.

Argument FOR

"Equal Sacrifice." If the country is in danger, everyone should share the burden of defense, not just those willing to volunteer. It ensures the army has enough soldiers to win.

Argument AGAINST

"Loss of Liberty." Forcing someone to fight and potentially die against their will is a violation of their basic rights. Many argue a government shouldn't have that much power over a citizen.

Exemptions

Not everyone is conscripted. Common exemptions include those with medical issues, essential workers (like farmers producing food), and Conscientious Objectors (people who refuse to fight for religious or moral reasons).

Reading in Context: The Crisis of 1917

By 1917, World War I had become a deadly war of attrition. Canada had lost so many men in battles like Vimy Ridge that the number of volunteers couldn't keep up with the casualties. Prime Minister Robert Borden, who had promised not to use conscription, felt he had no choice but to break his promise to keep the army strong.

He passed the Military Service Act, making enlistment mandatory. This tore the country apart. English-Canadians generally supported it, believing Canada had to support Britain. French-Canadians deeply opposed it; they felt no loyalty to the British Empire and believed it wasn't their war. Riots broke out in Quebec. While conscription did send 24,000 more soldiers to the front, it left a scar of division between English and French Canada that lasted for decades.
