Genetics

Breaking Down the Word: A Morphemic Analysis

genesis+-ics

Genesis: A Greek word meaning "origin" or "creation."

-ics: A suffix meaning "the study of" or "the science of."

So, "genetics" is literally the science of origins—the study of how traits are inherited.

The Core Idea: The Instruction Manual for Life

Genetics is the branch of biology that studies heredity—how physical and behavioural traits, like eye colour or personality, are passed down from one generation to the next. It explores the chemical instructions that make every living thing unique.

The Building Blocks of Heredity

Think of it like a library of cookbooks for building a person.

Chromosomes: The Bookshelves

Found in the nucleus of almost every cell, chromosomes are tightly coiled structures that organize all of your genetic information. Humans have 23 pairs of them.

DNA: The Cookbooks

Each chromosome is made of a long molecule called DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid). DNA is shaped like a twisted ladder (a double helix) and contains all the instructions needed for an organism to develop, survive, and reproduce.

Genes: The Individual Recipes

A gene is a specific section of DNA that holds the instructions for one particular trait, like the recipe for making blue eyes or curly hair.

Reading in Context: The Science Fair Project

For her science fair project, Sarah decided to investigate why her pea plants had different coloured flowers. Some were purple, and some were white. Her research led her to the work of Gregor Mendel, the "father of genetics." She learned that a specific gene determines flower colour, and this gene comes in two versions, or alleles: one for purple and one for white.

She discovered that the purple allele was "dominant." This meant that if a plant inherited even one copy of the purple gene, its flowers would be purple. The only way for a plant to have white flowers was to inherit two copies of the "recessive" white gene. By understanding these basic principles of genetics, Sarah could predict the flower colours of the next generation of plants, showing how traits are passed down in a predictable way.
