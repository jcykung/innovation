Pacifist

Breaking Down the Term

Pax+Facere

Pax: The Latin word for "peace."

Facere: The Latin verb meaning "to make."

So, a "pacifist" is literally a "peace maker."

The Core Idea: Opposition to War

A pacifist is someone who believes that war and violence are unjustifiable. They believe that conflicts between nations should be settled by peaceful means, such as diplomacy, negotiation, or non-violent protest, rather than by fighting.

Types of Pacifism

Not all pacifists think exactly alike. The degree of their opposition to violence can vary.

Absolute Pacifism

The belief that violence is always wrong, even in self-defense or to protect others. An absolute pacifist would refuse to kill under any circumstance.

Conditional Pacifism

The belief that war is generally bad, but might be the "lesser of two evils" in extreme cases (e.g., fighting to stop a genocide or a brutal dictator).

Religious Pacifism

Many religious groups, such as the Mennonites, Quakers, and Doukhobors, are pacifists because their interpretation of their faith strictly forbids killing.

Reading in Context: Conscientious Objectors in WWI

During World War I, the pressure to enlist in the army was enormous. Men who refused to fight were often called cowards or given white feathers as a symbol of shame. However, thousands of men refused to join the military because they were pacifists. These men were known as "Conscientious Objectors."

In Canada, many Conscientious Objectors came from religious communities like the Mennonites and Doukhobors. They argued that their moral duty to God ("Thou shall not kill") was higher than their duty to the government. The government treated them harshly. When conscription was introduced in 1917, many pacifists were stripped of their right to vote, and some were imprisoned for refusing to wear a uniform or hold a weapon. Their stand highlights the difficult conflict between personal ethics and national demands during a crisis.
