Heterozygous

Breaking Down the Word: A Morphemic Analysis

hetero-+-zygous

Hetero-: A Greek prefix meaning "different" or "other."

-zygous: From the Greek word "zygon," meaning a "yoke" or "pair."

So, "heterozygous" literally means "a different pair."

The Core Idea: Inheriting Two Different Alleles

An organism is heterozygous for a specific gene when it has inherited two different alleles (versions) for that geneâ€”one from each parent. Typically, this means inheriting one dominant and one recessive allele.

The "Carrier" Concept

A heterozygous individual is often called a "carrier" of the recessive trait. They don't show the trait, but they can pass the recessive allele on to their children.

Homozygous (Same Alleles)

Has two identical alleles.
Genotypes: BB or bb

Heterozygous (Different Alleles)

Has two different alleles.
Genotype: Bb

In a heterozygous genotype (Bb), the dominant 'B' allele masks the recessive 'b' allele. The person will show the dominant phenotype (Brown Eyes) but still carries the genetic code for blue eyes.

Reading in Context: The Brown-Eyed Parents

Two brown-eyed parents have a child with blue eyes. This might seem surprising, but it's a perfect example of how being heterozygous works. The blue-eye allele (b) is recessive, and the brown-eye allele (B) is dominant.

For their child to have blue eyes (genotype bb), the child must have inherited a 'b' allele from each parent. This means both brown-eyed parents must have a heterozygous genotype (Bb). They each show the brown-eyed phenotype, but they are both carriers for the blue-eyed allele, which they passed on to their child.
