Allele

Breaking Down the Word: A Morphemic Analysis

allelomorph

The word "allele" is a shortened form of "allelomorph," which comes from the Greek word "allos," meaning "other."

So, an "allele" is one of the "other" forms or versions of the same gene.

The Core Idea: A Different Version of a Gene

Most genes come in slightly different versions. An allele is one of these specific versions. For example, the gene for eye colour has several versions—an allele for brown eyes, an allele for blue eyes, etc. You inherit one allele for each gene from each parent.

Chromosomes

How Alleles Interact

The combination of alleles you inherit determines your traits.

Dominant vs. Recessive

A dominant allele will show its effect even if you only have one copy. A recessive allele will only show its effect if you inherit two copies.

Homozygous vs. Heterozygous

If you inherit two of the same alleles (e.g., two for blue eyes), you are homozygous for that trait. If you get two different alleles (one for brown, one for blue), you are heterozygous.

Reading in Context: The Earlobe Experiment

In science class, Maria learned that the gene for earlobes has two main versions. One allele is for detached earlobes (where the lobe hangs free), and the other allele is for attached earlobes (where it connects directly to the side of the head). The teacher explained that the detached earlobe allele is dominant.

Maria has detached earlobes, but her brother has attached ones. "That means your brother must have inherited two copies of the recessive allele for attached earlobes—one from Mom and one from Dad," the teacher said. "You, however, must have inherited at least one dominant allele for detached lobes. You can't be sure if you have two dominant alleles or just one, but that single allele is enough to give you that trait!"
