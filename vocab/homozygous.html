Homozygous

Breaking Down the Word: A Morphemic Analysis

homo-+-zygous

Homo-: A Greek prefix meaning "same."

-zygous: From the Greek word "zygon," meaning a "yoke" or "pair."

So, "homozygous" literally means "a matching pair."

The Core Idea: Inheriting Two Identical Alleles

An organism is homozygous for a specific gene when it has inherited two of the exact same alleles (versions) for that gene from its parents. One identical allele comes from each parent.

Two Types of Homozygous

Being homozygous can mean having two dominant alleles or two recessive alleles.

Homozygous Dominant

The organism has two copies of the dominant allele.
Genotype: BB
Phenotype: Dominant Trait (e.g., Brown Eyes)

Homozygous Recessive

The organism has two copies of the recessive allele. This is the only way a recessive trait can be seen.
Genotype: bb
Phenotype: Recessive Trait (e.g., Blue Eyes)

This is different from being heterozygous, which means having two different alleles for a gene (e.g., Bb).

Reading in Context: Purebred Puppies

A dog breeder specializes in "purebred" golden retrievers with a specific light-gold coat. To guarantee this colour, she must ensure her dogs are homozygous for the recessive allele that produces that shade. If a dog carried a dominant allele for a darker coat, it wouldn't be the right colour.

When she breeds two of her light-gold retrievers, all the puppies are also light-gold. This is because both parents have a homozygous recessive genotype (for example, 'gg'). Since they only have 'g' alleles to pass on, all of their puppies will also have a 'gg' genotype and show the same light-gold coat. There is no chance of a surprise darker puppy appearing.
